Frequently Asked Questions About the AFL Fixture

The AFL fixture generates countless questions from fans each season, particularly those new to Australian football or following from overseas. Understanding how the fixture works, when it's released, and how to access it effectively can significantly enhance your viewing experience. Below are answers to the most common questions about AFL fixtures, covering everything from basic scheduling to complex finals scenarios.

Whether you're a longtime supporter or new to the game, these answers provide practical information about accessing fixtures, understanding the season structure, and planning your viewing around the 23-round home-and-away season plus finals. The AFL's unique scheduling approach differs from most American sports leagues, making these explanations valuable for US-based fans adjusting to Australian football's rhythms.

When is the AFL fixture released each year?

The AFL fixture is typically released in October or November for the following season, usually around 8-10 months before the opening round. The 2024 fixture was released in early November 2023, giving fans, clubs, and broadcasters substantial lead time for planning. The release timing allows clubs to organize membership campaigns, arrange travel logistics, and coordinate with venue operators. The fixture announcement has become a significant media event, broadcast live on AFL.com.au and covered extensively across Australian sports media. The AFL Commission finalizes the fixture after months of consultation with clubs, broadcasters, stadium managers, and other stakeholders. While the complete 23-round schedule is released at once, minor adjustments occasionally occur throughout the season due to weather, stadium availability, or broadcast requirements, though such changes are relatively rare and typically announced weeks in advance.

How many games are in the AFL fixture?

The AFL regular season fixture consists of 207 games across 23 rounds, with each of the 18 teams playing 22 games before the finals series. This total has remained consistent since the league stabilized at 18 teams in 2012. The finals series adds between 8-9 additional matches depending on whether a Grand Final draw occurs (extremely rare, with the last draw in 2010 before rule changes made draws nearly impossible). Each round typically features nine matches, though bye rounds in the middle of the season see reduced match numbers as teams take scheduled breaks. The 207-game regular season represents a massive logistical undertaking spanning approximately 24 weeks from early March through late August. For comparison, the NFL regular season features 272 games across 18 weeks with 32 teams, making the AFL's fixture density and geographic spread across an entire continent particularly challenging to coordinate.

Where can I find the official AFL fixture?

The official AFL fixture is available on AFL.com.au, which serves as the authoritative source for all scheduling information including dates, times, venues, and broadcast details. The AFL's official mobile app, available for iOS and Android, provides the same fixture information with added functionality like push notifications for your followed teams, calendar integration, and automatic time zone conversion. For US-based fans, the Watch AFL international streaming service includes comprehensive fixture information with times displayed in your local zone. Individual club websites also publish their specific fixtures with additional context about opponents and travel schedules. Many fans download PDF versions of the complete fixture for offline reference, available from AFL.com.au shortly after the official release. Third-party sports apps and websites like ESPN also carry AFL fixture information, though the official AFL sources remain most reliable for last-minute changes or updates.

How does the AFL determine which teams play each other twice?

The AFL uses a complex formula considering multiple factors to determine which teams face each other twice in the fixture. Recent performance is weighted heavily, with teams finishing in similar ladder positions more likely to play twice, theoretically creating competitive balance. Traditional rivalries are protected regardless of performance, ensuring matches like Collingwood-Carlton, Essendon-Hawthorn, and Adelaide-Port Adelaide occur twice annually. Geographic considerations matter significantly, with Victorian clubs typically playing each other twice to minimize travel costs and maximize local derbies. Broadcast appeal influences decisions, as high-drawing matchups generate better television ratings and attendance. Stadium availability and capacity also factor in, particularly for clubs sharing venues. The AFL uses proprietary software to model thousands of potential fixture variations before selecting the optimal combination balancing these competing interests. This process lacks complete transparency, leading to regular complaints from clubs and fans about perceived inequities, though the AFL maintains that perfect balance is mathematically impossible with 18 teams playing 22 games each.

What is the difference between AFL finals and regular season fixtures?

AFL finals fixtures differ fundamentally from regular season scheduling in structure, stakes, and timing. The finals feature only the top eight teams from the regular season ladder, competing in a four-week playoff using the final-eight system that provides second chances for higher-ranked teams. Unlike the regular season where fixture matchups are predetermined, finals matchups depend entirely on ladder positions and results from previous finals. Venue allocation shifts heavily toward major stadiums, with the MCG contractually guaranteed to host both preliminary finals and the Grand Final regardless of participating teams. Finals matches typically occur on Friday nights, Saturday afternoons/evenings, and occasionally Thursday nights, avoiding the Sunday timeslots common during the regular season. The stakes escalate dramatically, as every finals match is elimination for at least one team (except qualifying finals where losers get second chances). Finals receive enhanced broadcast coverage, larger crowds, and significantly more media attention. Ticket pricing increases substantially, with Grand Final tickets among Australia's most sought-after sporting events, often selling for thousands of dollars on secondary markets.

Can I download a printable AFL fixture for 2024?

Yes, printable PDF versions of the complete 2024 AFL fixture are available for download from AFL.com.au shortly after the official release each November. These PDFs typically include several format options: a complete fixture showing all 207 games organized by round, team-specific fixtures showing each club's 22 games with opponents and venues, and venue-based fixtures showing all matches at specific stadiums. Most PDFs include spaces for noting scores or personal annotations, maintaining the tradition of printed fixture cards that Australian football fans have used for decades. Individual club websites also offer printable fixtures featuring their team's schedule with club branding and additional information about membership benefits or travel packages. For US-based fans, some websites create modified fixture PDFs with times converted to American time zones, though these are typically unofficial productions from fan communities rather than AFL-sanctioned materials. The PDF fixtures are sized for standard printing, allowing fans to post them on refrigerators, office walls, or carry folded copies, despite the increasing dominance of digital fixture access through apps and websites.

Why do some AFL teams get more Friday night games than others?

Friday night fixture allocation reflects a combination of performance, market size, membership base, and broadcast value that creates significant disparities between clubs. Friday night is the AFL's premium timeslot, typically drawing the largest television audiences and best attendance figures, making these matches most valuable to broadcasters who pay billions for AFL rights. Traditionally strong clubs with large fanbases like Collingwood, Richmond, Essendon, and Carlton receive disproportionate Friday night allocations because they reliably deliver high ratings and crowds. Recent on-field success also influences allocation, as the AFL and broadcasters prefer competitive, high-quality matches in premium slots. Victorian clubs have geographic advantages, as Friday night matches at the MCG or Marvel Stadium avoid the complications of interstate travel and time zone differences. Smaller market clubs, particularly interstate teams outside major cities, receive fewer Friday night matches despite regular complaints about fixture inequity. The AFL has implemented minimum guarantees ensuring every club receives at least some prime-time exposure, but significant gaps remain. This creates financial implications, as Friday night exposure boosts membership, sponsorship value, and merchandise sales, potentially widening the gap between rich and poor clubs.

How do bye rounds work in the AFL fixture?

Bye rounds are scheduled breaks where teams don't play, strategically placed in the AFL fixture to manage player workload and extend the season's broadcast footprint. The current system typically features three bye rounds (rounds 12-14) where six teams rest each week, ensuring every team gets one bye during the season while maintaining nine matches across those three rounds. This structure replaced earlier formats including a split round where all teams had byes simultaneously. The bye placement around mid-season provides players crucial recovery time during the grueling 23-round campaign, addressing sports science concerns about injury risk and fatigue accumulation. Clubs use bye weeks for player rehabilitation, tactical review, and sometimes promotional activities or community engagement without match-day pressures. From a broadcast perspective, bye rounds extend the season's calendar footprint without reducing total content, maintaining continuous AFL coverage across more weeks. The fixture ensures teams don't receive byes before or after already-scheduled breaks like representative matches or public holidays. Some teams perceive bye timing as advantageous or disadvantageous depending on their injury situation and upcoming opponents, adding another dimension to fixture equity debates that persist throughout each season.
